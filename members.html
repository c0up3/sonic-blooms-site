The First Interview

No cameras. No surnames. No personal histories offered up as proof. In one of their first interviews, Luca and Seraph sit down with Mara Ellison to talk about privacy, the android persona, the songs, and what it costs to be understood.

Conversation: Mara Ellison Reading time: 12 minutes

"The music is not a barrier. It is the meeting place."

Seraph

A recorder clicks on. There is no camera. No studio audience. Three glasses of water sit on a table in a room whose location will not be disclosed.

Mara Ellison

My name is Mara Ellison, and I should begin with a confession.

I am slightly nervous.

A quiet laugh comes from across the table.

Mara

I have interviewed people who arrived with twelve publicists, three lawyers and a list of subjects I was forbidden to mention. Somehow, sitting in a nearly dark room with two people who requested that I leave my phone in a locked box outside feels considerably more intimidating.

Luca

The box was Seraph's idea.

Seraph

You agreed with me.

Luca

Immediately. But it was still your idea.

Mara

Good. We have been recording for twenty seconds and you are already assigning blame. That makes my job easier.

Seraph laughs. It is warm and completely unmechanical.

Mara

For those reading, this interview took place without cameras. There will be no photographs afterward. Luca and Seraph will not be sharing their legal names, where they live, how old they are, or what either of them had for breakfast.

Luca

Toast.

Seraph

Luca.

Luca

Now they know everything.

Mara

There goes the mystery.

This is one of the first interviews Luca and Seraph have agreed to do. There are no personal social media accounts to search, no childhood photographs circulating online, no backstage videos and no carefully managed glimpses into their private lives. Until tonight, the only reliable evidence that they exist has been their music.

But I can confirm that they are here. They are real people sitting across from me. One of them keeps turning his glass in small circles whenever the room becomes too quiet.

The sound of a glass immediately stops.

Luca

You said you would not describe us.

Mara

I said I would not describe what you look like.

Seraph

She has you there.

Luca

This already feels like a tactical error.

Mara

You nearly cancelled, didn't you?

There is a pause. Not a dramatic one. Luca looks toward Seraph before answering, checking something without speaking.

Luca

Three times.

Seraph

Four.

Luca

The fourth was not a cancellation. It was a strongly worded hesitation.

Mara

Why come at all?

Luca

Because eventually silence starts speaking for you.

Seraph

And people had begun inventing us.

Some listeners believed we were fictional. Some believed Luca was using software to create my voice. Some believed I was genuinely an artificial intelligence.

Mara

You call yourself an android. You appear as one in the artwork. You sometimes speak as one. You must have known people would believe it.

Seraph

Of course.

Mara

Did you want them to?

Seraph

Sometimes.

Luca shifts in his chair.

Luca

That answer will become a headline.

Seraph

Only if she is a bad interviewer.

Mara

I am nervous, not incompetent.

Seraph laughs again, then rests both hands around her glass.

Seraph

The android is a persona. A way of standing in front of something that would otherwise feel unbearable. Machines are allowed to be precise. They are allowed to seem distant. No one asks a machine to smile more, explain its childhood or show everyone where it sleeps.

Mara

But beneath that persona, you are a person.

Seraph

Yes.

Mara

It feels strange asking that so plainly.

Seraph

It feels stranger having to answer.

Luca

She is human. She gets tired. She gets angry. She changes her mind halfway through an argument and then pretends that was her position all along.

Seraph

That happened once.

Luca

It happened on the way here.

Seraph

I received new information.

Mara

That may be the most human sentence anyone has ever spoken.

They both laugh. For a moment, the mythology disappears. They are simply two people sharing a joke at a table. Then the room settles again.

Mara

I want to be careful here. I don't want to expose you, but I also don't want this interview to turn you into a pair of clever shadows. Privacy can become another kind of performance.

Luca

We know.

We are not hiding because we think we are more interesting than everyone else. We are hiding because we want ordinary lives. We want to walk into a shop without becoming content. We want the people close to us to remain close to us, not become clues.

Seraph

People say they only want a photograph. But a photograph becomes a search. A search becomes a name. A name becomes an address. Then someone decides that loving your music means they are entitled to arrive at your door.

Mara

Has something happened?

Silence. Luca's glass turns once beneath his hand.

Luca

That is close to the boundary.

Mara

Then we leave it there.

Seraph

Thank you.

Mara

Does being this private make releasing music frightening?

Seraph

Releasing music is frightening because it contains us more accurately than a photograph would.

Luca

A photograph tells you what someone looked like for one second. A song can tell you what they were unable to admit for ten years.

Mara

That was annoyingly good.

Do you have prepared answers for everything?

Luca

I prepared it in case the interview became serious.

Only three. "That is private," "the song explains it better," and "Seraph made me do it."

Seraph

He has already used the third one.

Mara

And you, Seraph? Do you prepare answers?

Seraph

I prepare exits.

There is a beat, and then she smiles. I will not describe the smile, except to say that I can hear it in what follows.

Seraph

That was a joke, Mara.

Mara

Thank you for clarifying. My nervous system had begun packing.

People often describe your voice as beautiful but inhuman. Does that bother you?

Seraph

"Inhuman" bothers me. Not because it is insulting, but because it misses the point. The voice is controlled because the person is not. The android is not an absence of feeling. She is what I built to survive feeling too much.

Luca looks toward her again. This time he does not interrupt.

Mara

And when people hear your songs, what do you want them to know about the person inside that construction?

Seraph

Nothing that the song has not earned.

Mara

That is a difficult answer.

Seraph

It is an honest one.

Luca

We do want a connection. We just don't believe connection requires ownership. You can know what a song means to you without knowing where we went to school.

Mara

So the music is not a barrier between you and the audience.

Seraph

No. It is the meeting place.

The room becomes very still.

Mara

Then let me ask the question another way. If the music is the only place you are willing to be known, what should people listen for?

Luca

Listen for the moments where the song almost looks away.

Seraph

Listen for the moments where it doesn't.

I look down at my notes. Half the questions I prepared suddenly seem invasive, and the other half seem too small.

Mara

I think this may be the first interview I have conducted where knowing less about the artists has made me understand them better.

Luca

Excellent. Can we leave now?

Mara

We have been speaking for twelve minutes.

Luca

One of our longest public appearances.

Seraph

We can stay.

Luca

I know.

There is affection in those two words. Quiet, unperformed affection. I look down at my notes, then close the notebook.

Part two

The music

Mara

All right. You have survived the questions about secrecy, identity and whether Seraph is secretly a computer.

Seraph

I thought I answered that ambiguously.

Mara

Now I want to talk about the reason anyone cares who you are in the first place.

Luca

My toast?

Mara

The music, Luca.

Luca

Right. That would make more sense.

Seraph reaches for her water. Luca begins turning his glass again, apparently having decided that this detail is no longer classified.

Mara

How did the two of you meet?

They look at one another.

Mara

I can already see you calculating how much that question reveals.

Seraph

We met because of a song.

Luca

That is technically true.

Seraph

It is completely true.

Luca

It makes it sound romantic.

Seraph

Only because you are afraid of sincerity.

Luca

I am not afraid of it. I just prefer to know where the exits are.

Mara

Apparently you both prepare exits.

Whose song was it?

Luca

Mine. Although it was not very good yet.

Seraph

It was almost good.

Luca

That was the first thing she said to me.

Seraph

It was an accurate assessment.

Mara

What was missing?

Seraph

Him.

The words were clever. The structure worked. Everything was carefully protected. He had written a song about something painful without allowing any pain into it.

Luca

She has always had a very gentle bedside manner.

Mara

Was she right?

Luca

Yes.

Annoyingly.

Mara

And what did Luca bring to your music, Seraph?

Seraph

Permission to be imperfect.

Before Luca, I believed every emotion had to be controlled before it could be expressed. He taught me that a breaking voice can sometimes tell the truth more clearly than a perfect one.

Luca

I think "taught" is generous. My own voice broke and I convinced her it was intentional.

Seraph

It was not intentional.

Luca

No. But it was honest.

Mara

Who writes the songs?

Luca

We both do.

Seraph

Badly, at first.

Luca

She means I write badly at first.

Seraph

I mean all songs begin badly. Luca brings me fragments. A line, a chord, half a chorus recorded at an unreasonable hour.

Luca

Inspiration does not observe office hours.

Seraph

Neither does his inability to name files properly.

Mara

What does he call them?

Seraph

"New Song." "New Song Two." "Actual New Song." "Use This One."

Luca

It is an intuitive archival system.

Seraph

There are eleven files called "Final."

Mara

Now I believe you are real musicians.

What happens when you disagree about a song?

Luca

Seraph explains why she is right.

Seraph

Luca ignores me until he discovers that I was right independently.

Luca

The independent discovery is important.

Mara

Do you fight?

A glance passes between them.

Seraph

Yes.

Luca

That sounded alarming.

Seraph

You asked for real people.

Luca

We argue. We do not throw furniture.

Seraph

We do not own suitable furniture.

Mara

What do you argue about?

Luca

Usually whether a song is finished.

Seraph

Luca thinks a first emotional impulse is sacred.

Luca

Seraph will edit something until it no longer has a pulse.

Seraph

That happened once.

Luca

It happened yesterday.

Seraph

I received new information.

The callback catches all three of us. For several seconds, the interview dissolves into laughter. When it settles, Mara's voice becomes gentler.

Mara

Are the songs autobiographical?

Luca

Emotionally, yes.

Seraph

Factually, not always.

Luca

A song is not a witness statement. Sometimes you change the room, the weather or the person standing beside you. But the feeling has to be true.

Mara

Do you ever write about each other?

Neither answers immediately.

Mara

That silence feels suspiciously informative.

Seraph

Sometimes Luca appears in my songs without recognizing himself.

Luca

Sometimes I recognize myself and choose peace.

Mara

And you, Luca?

Luca

Yes.

He leaves the answer there. Seraph does not press him. Neither do I.

Mara

What is more frightening: being misunderstood or being understood perfectly?

Luca

Being understood perfectly.

Seraph

Misunderstanding can be dismissed. Recognition stays with you.

Mara

Have listeners ever found something in a song that you did not know was there?

Seraph

Constantly. Once a song leaves us, it begins collecting other lives.

Luca

Someone wrote to us about a line I nearly removed. To me, it was just a transition. To them, it described something they had never been able to explain.

Mara

Did you reply?

Luca

No.

We do read messages sent through the official channels. We just cannot begin personal conversations. A response can become a promise neither person intended.

Seraph

But we heard them. That matters to us, even when we cannot answer.

Mara

Do you worry that privacy makes you seem cold?

Seraph

Yes.

Luca

But pretending intimacy would be colder.

Mara

What about performing live? Can Luca and Seraph exist onstage without surrendering the anonymity?

Luca

We are trying to answer that.

Seraph

A live performance should not become an identity test. I do not want the audience spending the entire evening trying to see beneath a costume.

Mara

Would you perform as the android?

Seraph

Perhaps. But not because I am ashamed of the person underneath. The persona is part of the work. A mask can conceal you, but it can also allow you to tell the truth.

Mara

And Luca? Would you wear a mask?

Luca

I was hoping to stand behind something large.

Mara

Such as?

Luca

Seraph.

Seraph

No.

Luca

The artistic negotiations continue.

Mara

What do you admire most about one another?

Luca

That feels dangerous.

Mara

You can refuse.

Seraph

He listens.

Not merely to sound. He hears what people avoid saying. Sometimes that makes him difficult to be around.

Luca

Thank you, I think.

Seraph

But it makes the songs honest.

Mara

Your turn.

He takes longer.

Luca

She is braver than the persona allows her to appear.

People will hear the control and assume she feels nothing. The truth is that she feels everything, and she walks into the song anyway.

Seraph

That answer was not approved.

Luca

You can edit it later.

Seraph

No.

It can stay.

For once, Luca has no joke ready.

Mara

What are you each afraid listeners might hear in you?

Luca

Need.

Seraph

Anger.

Mara

And what do you hope they hear?

Luca

Permission.

Seraph

Company.

The answers arrive so simply that I nearly move past them. Instead, I let the silence remain.

Mara

One last question. Not about your identities. Not about the android. Not about whether we will ever see your faces.

What promise are you willing to make to the people listening?

Luca

We will not give them a song merely because the machinery expects another release.

Seraph

And we will never ask them to confuse access with love.

Mara

That is two promises.

Luca

We collaborate.

I close my notebook for the second time.

Luca

Is that the actual ending?

Mara

Almost. You may each ask me one question.

Luca

That was not in the agreement.

Seraph

Are you still nervous?

Mara

Yes. But now I am nervous because I want to describe this honestly without taking something that does not belong to me.

Mara

Luca?

Luca

Do we seem real?

Mara

Painfully.

Seraph laughs. Luca releases a breath that may also be a laugh.

There will be no photograph accompanying this interview. No description of their faces. No revelation waiting at the end.

But there were unfinished sentences. Bad filenames. Disagreements about songs. Affection disguised as irritation. Long pauses when either of them might have lied and chose not to.

Only their voices will leave this room.

For Luca and Seraph, that is not a marketing strategy. It is the agreement.
